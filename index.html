About Me

I am a biostatistician with 8 years of experience in the design and analysis of clinical trials and real-world evidence (RWE) studies. My expertise lies in applying advanced statistical methodologies to generate meaningful insights in oncology, cardiology, infectious diseases, and healthcare outcomes research. I specialize in survival analysis, mixed-effects modeling, causal inference, and machine learning, with strong proficiency in SAS, R, and SQL.

I hold an MS in Biostatistics from Drexel University, a BS from Tribhuvan University, and a graduate certificate in Bioinformatics from the University of Delaware. Recently, I have been particularly interested in causal inference methods and their applications in healthcare economics and outcomes research, aiming to improve decision-making and patient care through rigorous data analysis.

My research has been published in high-impact journals, and I actively contribute to the scientific community as a member of the American Statistical Association (ASA), the International Biometric Society (ENAR), and the Association for Clinical and Translational Science (ACTS).

On the personal side, I am passionate about community service, volleyball, and hiking. I play for the Everest Volleyball Team, which was the defending champion of the City of Newark Winter Volleyball League (B Division). Beyond sports, I have a deep love for the outdoors and have hiked many peaks across the United States and Nepal, embracing the challenge and adventure that come with exploring nature.

Feel free to connect with me on LinkedIn or explore my research on Google Scholar.

Personal Life & Photos

I try to follow the Buddhist approach to understand and live life. I find it a very systematic and methodological way to look at life and live meaningfully. Buddhism proposes the Four Noble Truths as it comes to life:

Dukkha (Suffering Exists) – Life is filled with suffering, dissatisfaction, and impermanence. This includes physical pain, emotional distress, and the existential struggles of life.

Samudaya (Cause of Suffering) – The root of suffering is craving (tanha) and attachment, whether to desires, possessions, status, or even ideas. Our attachment leads to endless cycles of dissatisfaction.

Nirodha (Cessation of Suffering) – By letting go of attachment and craving, suffering can be ceased. Enlightenment (Nirvana) is the state of peace and liberation from suffering.

Magga (Path to the End of Suffering) – The way to end suffering is through the Eightfold Path, a disciplined and mindful way of living that leads to wisdom, ethical conduct, and mental discipline.

The Eightfold Path

The Eightfold Path is the practical guide to overcoming suffering and achieving enlightenment. It is divided into three categories: Wisdom (Prajna), Ethical Conduct (Sila), and Mental Discipline (Samadhi).

1. Wisdom (Prajna)

  • Right View (Samma Ditthi) – Understanding reality as it is, especially the Four Noble Truths. It is about seeing life without illusions.
  • Right Intention (Samma Sankappa) – Cultivating the right thoughts and intentions, free from ill will, cruelty, and attachment.

2. Ethical Conduct (Sila)

  • Right Speech (Samma Vaca) – Speaking truthfully, kindly, and constructively while avoiding gossip, lies, or harmful speech.
  • Right Action (Samma Kammanta) – Acting ethically by avoiding harm to others, refraining from stealing, and leading a moral life.
  • Right Livelihood (Samma Ajiva) – Earning a living in an honest and ethical way, without harming others or exploiting beings.

3. Mental Discipline (Samadhi)

  • Right Effort (Samma Vayama) – Diligently cultivating positive states of mind and eliminating negative ones.
  • Right Mindfulness (Samma Sati) – Being aware of one's thoughts, actions, and surroundings with clarity and presence.
  • Right Concentration (Samma Samadhi) – Developing deep states of meditation to cultivate wisdom and inner peace.
